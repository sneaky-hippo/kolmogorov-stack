The closed-API tax.

Every AI feature you ship over a closed API is a tax you cannot stop paying. It scales with usage, compounds per customer, and is repriced on a 14-day notice. The only honest exit is a compiled artifact you own.

A note on the name.

Kolmogorov asked in 1965 what the smallest specialist program producing a given behavior is. The CLI is kolm like gcc or git. The artifact is .kolm like .tar or .deb. The spec is RS-1, MIT-licensed; the company is steward, not gatekeeper.

What the tax actually is.

An AI-native SaaS at Series A scale: twelve features touching a frontier API; two of them (classification and extraction) account for 60% of calls and 80% of the model bill. Together: $160,000 a year on a $0.04-per-call line item, growing linearly with paying customers, on a metric the vendor invented and changes at will.

That is the tax. The remaining 5% (genuinely hard, frontier-class reasoning) is fine. The 95% of predictable, repeated, deterministic-shape calls running through a meter you do not control is not.

Not theoretical. Three design partners audited their model bills at our request. Compile-eligible spend ranged 47%-81% of total. The remainder is where frontier APIs earn their margin.

Who pays it.

  • AI-native SaaS. Every AI feature has a per-customer variable cost; gross margin is a function of calls per customer. Your AWS bill compounds; your model bill compounds harder.
  • Vertical software with AI bolted on. The CFO underlines the model line when it crosses 6%. You priced the feature once; the vendor reprices inference quarterly.
  • Regulated buyers paying twice. Healthcare, finance, defense pay the per-call tax and the BAA review tax: every cloud call is an audit row. The tax has a privacy surcharge.

Why the tax persists.

1. The model is a black box.

Until recently you could not measure a model's behavior on your specific tasks in a way you could defend at review. The K-score fixes this: one number on the cover, five you can defend, gates a deploy the way a test suite gates a build.

2. Open weights were not good enough.

For three years the answer was "almost." Qwen-2.5-3B and Llama-3-8B in 2026 are good enough for the deterministic 80% with a LoRA on your eval set, drafted by recipes for structured output, grounded via Recall. The student only has to match the teacher on your tasks.

3. The compile step was missing.

No build step turned your task + your data + your eval set into a signed artifact in one command. Distillation was a research artifact; fine-tuning a vendor-locked feature; runtimes and evaluators were separate concerns. kolm puts them in one CLI.

kolm compile is the build step that turns the rented 80% into something you own.

What the exit looks like.

Same SaaS, same 4M ticket-classification calls/mo. Before: $160k/yr, variable, vendor-controlled. After:

  • One compile. $700 in teacher-API spend to k-sample and verify the labeled set. One day of engineering to vet the eval pairs and ship through CI.
  • One serve. $99/mo on a single H100 sliver, A10, L40, or 5090. Same artifact across all.
  • One signed receipt per call. Free, verifiable offline, optionally anchored to Sigstore Rekor at no charge.

Total after migration: $1,888/yr. Same K-score band, lower latency, fixed cost. Frontier API stays for the 5% of hard calls.

The objection we get most.

"Why not just use a smaller model from the same vendor?"

Same tax structure, different rate. Vendor still deprecates models on 14-day notice and owns your inference path. Switching Sonnet to Haiku is a discount on rent. Compiling is buying the building.

What the open contract looks like.

Artifact format, receipt chain, K-score formula: open. The CLI is open in shape; the compiler is the proprietary service because the compile flywheel is the moat. What you compile is yours. If we vanish tomorrow, your shipped artifacts keep running. We are a dependency on the build step, not the runtime.

Runtime is whatever fits: llama.cpp on a server, ExecuTorch on a phone, a browser adapter, a Jetson. Same artifact, same receipt, same verifier.

The tax was the price of the cloud not having a build step. Now it has one.

What you do on Monday.

Pull last quarter's model bill. Sort by feature. The two or three features eating half the spend are almost certainly classification, extraction, or rewrite. Pick the one with the cleanest eval set. Compile it. Run a week of A/B against the K-score gate. Cut over.

One feature, one quarter, one number on the unit-economics line. Then the next.

Cloud AI got smart by sending your users' data away. Compiled AI gets smart by leaving it there.
