Manifesto · 2026-05-09

The closed-API tax.

Every AI feature you ship over a closed API is a tax you cannot stop paying. It scales with your usage. It compounds with every customer you add. It is set by a vendor whose pricing is a 14-day notice. This is the argument that the tax is real, that it is bigger than it looks, and that the only honest exit is a compiled artifact you own.

A note on the name.

We chose Kolmogorov because the question we are answering (what is the smallest specialist program that produces a given behavior) is exactly what Andrey Kolmogorov asked in 1965. The CLI is kolm because, like gcc or git, the binary is small enough to type. The artifact is .kolm because, like .tar or .deb, the file is the unit of distribution. The spec is RS-1, MIT-licensed, so anyone can implement a compatible compiler. The company is the steward, not the gatekeeper. More on the brand stack →

What the tax actually is.

Take any AI-native SaaS at Series A scale. Twelve features touching a frontier API. Two of them (classification and extraction) account for 60% of the calls and 80% of the model bill. Together they are $160,000 a year on a $0.04-per-call line item, growing linearly with paying customers, charged on a metric the vendor invented and the vendor changes at will.

That number is the tax. It is not the rest of the model bill, the part where you call frontier reasoning for the genuinely hard 5% of queries. That part is fine. What is not fine is the 95% (the predictable, repeated, deterministic-shape calls) running through a meter you do not control.

The remainder (the genuinely hard, genuinely creative reasoning) is where frontier APIs earn their margin. The tax is the share you should never have been paying in the first place.

Who pays it.

Three groups, each in a slightly different shape.

  • AI-native SaaS. The most direct sufferer. Every AI feature in your product has a per-customer variable cost. Your gross margin is a function of how many calls each customer makes. Your AWS bill compounds. Your model bill compounds harder.
  • Vertical software with AI features bolted on. Slightly cushioned by the rest of the contract, but the model bill is the line item the CFO underlines when it crosses 6%. Renewals get harder as the AI usage scales. You priced the feature once. The vendor reprices their inference quarterly.
  • Regulated buyers paying twice. Healthcare, finance, defense. They pay the per-call tax and the BAA review tax: every cloud call is a discoverable artifact, a audit row, a compliance investigation waiting to happen. The tax has a privacy surcharge.

Why the tax persists.

Three reasons. Each defensible. None permanent.

1. The model is a black box.

You cannot ship something you cannot measure, and until recently you could not measure a model's behavior on your specific tasks in a way you could defend at a review. The K-score fixes this. One number on the cover, five you can defend; gates a deploy the way a test suite gates a build.

2. Open weights were not good enough.

For three years the answer to "can the open model do this?" was "almost." Almost is not shippable. Qwen-2.5-3B and Llama-3-8B in 2026 are good enough for the deterministic 80%, especially with a LoRA fit on your specific eval set, drafted by recipes that cover the structured output, and grounded in your data via Recall. The student does not have to match the teacher in the open domain. It only has to match the teacher on your tasks.

3. The compile step was missing.

Even when the model existed and the score existed, there was no build step that turned your task + your data + your eval set into a signed artifact in a single command. There was distillation as a research artifact, fine-tuning as a vendor-locked feature, runtimes as a separate concern, evaluators as a side project. kolm puts them in one CLI.

The tax persists because there was no exit. Now there is. kolm compile is the build step that turns the rented 80% into something you own.

What the exit looks like.

Same Series A SaaS. Same 4M ticket-classification calls per month. Before the exit: $160k/yr, variable, growing, vendor-controlled. After the exit:

  • One compile. $700 in teacher-API spend to k-sample and verify a labeled set. One day of engineering time to vet the eval pairs and ship the artifact through CI.
  • One serve. $99/mo on a single H100 sliver, an A10, an L40, or a 5090. The same artifact runs across all of them.
  • One signed receipt per call. Free. Verifiable offline. Anchored to Sigstore Rekor at no charge if you want third-party-verifiable provenance.

Total cost after migration: $1,888/yr. Same K-score band, same accuracy, lower latency, fixed cost. The frontier API is still there for the 5% of genuinely-hard calls. The rest moved.

The objection we get most.

"Why not just use a smaller model from the same vendor?"

Because the smaller model is the same tax structure with a different rate. Vendor still controls the meter. Vendor still deprecates models on a 14-day notice. Vendor still owns your inference path. Switching from Sonnet to Haiku is not an exit; it is a discount on the rent. Compiling is buying the building.

What the open contract looks like.

The artifact format is open. The receipt chain is open. The K-score formula is open. The CLI is open in shape; the compiler is the proprietary service because the compile flywheel is the moat. What you compile is yours. If we go away tomorrow, the artifacts you've shipped keep running. Your customers keep getting answers. Your CI keeps building. We are a dependency on the build step, not a dependency on the runtime.

The runtime is whatever runtime fits: llama.cpp on a server, ExecuTorch on a phone, a browser adapter, a Jetson. The artifact is the same. The receipt is the same. The verifier is the same.

The tax was the price of the cloud not having a build step. kolm compile is the build step. The artifact is what you keep.

What you do on Monday.

Pull last quarter's model bill. Sort by feature. Find the two or three features that account for half the spend; they are almost certainly classification, extraction, or rewrite. Pick the one with the cleanest eval set you already have. Compile it. Run a week of A/B traffic against the K-score gate. Cut over.

One feature, one quarter, one number on the unit-economics line. Then the next.

Cloud AI got smart by sending your users' data away. Compiled AI gets smart by leaving it there. The tax was the price of the cloud not having a build step. Now it has one.
