self-host · sovereign-cloud kolm

Run the entire kolm stack in your environment.

Registry, compiler, receipts service. One Helm chart, three pods, your network. No call goes out unless you punt it out. Same .kolm format the open runtime executes. Same K-score floor. Same signed receipts. Your secret, your boundary.

architecture

Three pods. Your network.

The full kolm control plane fits inside three deployments. None of them reach the public internet unless you let them. The compiler can be air-gapped at runtime; the registry indexes only what you publish to it; the receipts service holds the HMAC secret you rotate.

All three pods are stateless. State lives in your object store and your postgres. Egress out of the VPC is off by default; the runtime emits no telemetry to kolm.ai.

when to self-host

Pick this when the data cannot leave.

SaaS at kolm.ai is the right call for ninety-five percent of workloads. Self-host kicks in when the compliance or audit boundary says nothing leaves this network, the data the model touches is classified, or the buyer mandates sovereign cloud.

regulated workloads

Healthcare with PHI

Hospital systems, payer ops, claims engines. The compiler sits next to your data; the recipe is signed in your network; receipts archive into your audit system. No PHI ever leaves the VPC, including during compile.

classified data

Defense and intelligence

Air-gapped enclaves, IL5 / IL6 environments, multi-level security boundaries. The compiler ships as a single static binary plus the registry chart; no external dependency at compile time once the base model is mirrored locally.

audit-trail requirements

Tier 1 finance

Regulators want every model decision attributable to a signed recipe and a signed receipt. Self-host keeps the HMAC secret inside your KMS and the receipt archive inside your bucket. The auditor reads them with the open verifier.

sovereign-cloud mandate

EU, UK, AU public sector

Procurement clauses that require data residency, sovereign-cloud hosting, or full source-code escrow. Run the open runtime under your terms. The MIT licence applies to the runtime; the control-plane charts are licensed for self-host use.

requirements

What you bring.

Minimum production-grade footprint. The numbers below are targets for the v1 chart; a smaller pilot runs comfortably on a three-node k3s cluster.

kubernetes
v1.27 or newer. EKS, GKE, AKS, OpenShift, or vanilla k3s. Three pods plus a job-runner namespace.
postgres
v14 or newer. ~5 GB for the tenant + job ledger at 10k recipes/month. Managed RDS, CloudSQL, or in-cluster Crunchy operator all supported.
object store
S3 API-compatible. AWS S3, GCS via interop, Azure Blob via gateway, MinIO for fully on-prem. Holds .kolm artifacts and signed receipts.
secrets
KMS for the HMAC receipt secret. AWS KMS, GCP KMS, HashiCorp Vault, or any KMIP store. Rotated per the policy you define.
base models
You mirror the base model weights into your network (HuggingFace mirror or air-gap import). The compiler never reaches the public internet at runtime once weights are local.
observability
Standard Prometheus metrics, OpenTelemetry traces, JSON structured logs. Plug into your existing stack; nothing exfiltrates.
delta from SaaS

What changes when you self-host.

The runtime, spec, and CLI are unchanged. The artifact format is unchanged. The K-score floor is unchanged. The difference is the boundary.

control plane
In your VPC. Endpoint is your DNS, e.g. kolm.internal.your-corp.cloud.
receipts
Signed with your HMAC secret in your KMS. Independent verification with your secret; same shape as the SaaS receipt.
registry
Private to your network. Federated pull from the public kolm.ai catalog is optional and opt-in per recipe.
support
Direct line to engineering. SLA tied to the contract. Quarterly review against the public roadmap.
billing
Annual license. Per-cluster or per-node. No per-call metering; you run as many compiles as the cluster handles.
