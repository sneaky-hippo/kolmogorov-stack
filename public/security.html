03decided not to pursue · with reasons

What we will not pretend to.

A short list of frameworks our buyers occasionally ask about and we have decided not to pursue. Each row says why. Saying it up front saves a procurement cycle.

CMMC Level 1+not pursuing

Defense and government are not our ICP.

CMMC compliance overhead would force pricing changes that hurt our healthcare, finance, and legal buyers. If you need a defense-controlled environment, run kolm self-hosted on your own GovCloud or on-prem infrastructure. The runtime supports it; we will not attest to CMMC ourselves.

FedRAMPnot pursuing

Same reasoning as CMMC.

FedRAMP Moderate or High is a multi-year, seven-figure program built for vendors whose ICP is federal civilian or DoD. That is not us. Government primes can deploy the runtime air-gapped and bring their own ATO. Defense deployment notes.

ISO 27001not pursuing today

SOC 2 Type II overlaps about 80 percent.

Evidenced answer: SOC 2 Type II covers most of what ISO 27001 covers, US buyers ask for SOC 2 vastly more often than ISO, and dual-attesting costs about 1.6x for the second one. We will revisit post-SOC 2 if EU buyers consistently request it.

PCI-DSSnot applicable

We never touch payment data.

Stripe handles all cardholder PII for billing. The kolm control plane sees a Stripe customer ID and a price plan, nothing more. Out of scope by design, not by attestation gap. See Stripe row.

06key rotation lifecycle · wave 189 · rotate verb shipped wave 193

Key rotation lifecycle.

Every .kolm artifact is signed with Ed25519 (RS-1 v2.1 §7.9) and optionally bound to a Sigstore + Rekor transparency entry (RS-1 v2.1 §7.10). The signature scheme is cryptographically open-ended — Ed25519 keys do not expire mathematically — but operational keys must rotate on cadence for compliance, on demand for compromise, and on personnel change. This section names the three triggers, the verifier behavior during the overlap window, the CLI surface, and the four customer-hosted KMS export targets kolm interoperates with.

Why operational keys must rotate.

Ed25519 has no built-in expiry; the same private key can sign for decades without weakening. Operational policy is what forces rotation. NIST SP 800-57 Part 1 Rev 5 recommends cryptoperiods of one to two years for high-assurance signing keys; SOC 2 CC6.1 requires a documented, customer-defined rotation cadence with evidence of enforcement; HIPAA Security Rule 45 CFR 164.312(a)(2)(iv) and 164.312(d) demand encryption key management procedures including rotation, escrow, and revocation. Different jurisdictions add their own requirements for PHI or PII signing keys. kolm ships rotation primitives so the operator can satisfy whichever rule applies; kolm does not pick the cadence or host the keys.

Three rotation triggers.

Every rotation event names a reason. The reason becomes a field on the new key's directory entry and is propagated into the next compiled artifact, so a verifier walking the receipt chain can see when each generation was rotated in and why.

cadence

Scheduled rotation

Default cadence is 365 days, customer-configurable down to 30 days or up to the NIST SP 800-57 ceiling of 730 days. The previous key remains valid for an overlap window so in-flight signatures keep verifying.

primitive · kolm keys list shows age + days_to_rotate
compromise

Ad-hoc on suspected compromise

Triggered by your incident-response playbook the moment a key is suspected leaked. Drives the rotation immediately, marks the prior key as revoked rather than retired, and emits a compromise reason that disables the overlap window for that key.

primitive · reason=compromise revokes the prior key
personnel

Personnel change

HR-triggered when a key-holder departs, changes role, or loses access. Same mechanics as compromise (immediate revocation) but the reason carries audit-friendly context so the rotation is attributable to the policy, not to a security event.

primitive · reason=personnel with departing actor's fingerprint

What rotation does to existing artifacts.

An artifact signed with the old key keeps its original Ed25519 signature forever; the signature does not expire when the key is rotated. The verifier looks up the public key by the fingerprint embedded in the receipt (logical field signature.key_id; implemented as signature_ed25519.key_fingerprint in src/ed25519.js) and re-checks the signature against the on-disk receipt body. As long as the directory still publishes the public key, the artifact verifies.

During the overlap window (default 30 days, customer-configurable), both the prior key and the new key resolve to live entries in the public-key directory (src/pubkey-directory.js, Wave 149). Artifacts compiled before the rotation continue to verify against the prior key for the full lifetime of the artifact; artifacts compiled after the rotation verify against the new key. Verifier check #5 (Receipt signature, Ed25519, public-key) and check #17 (Signature policy, Ed25519) pass for either key as long as the fingerprint resolves in the directory. The overlap window is the operational grace period, not a cryptographic deadline.

CLI flow.

shipped

kolm keys list

Lists every key in the local signing store and the published public-key directory. Shows fingerprint, label, registered_at, days_in_service, and days_to_rotate against the configured cadence. Pairs with the existing kolm pubkey verb (Wave 149) that prints the active signing key's fingerprint and PEM.

shipped · reads ~/.kolm/signing-key.pem + data/keys-public.json
shipped · wave 193

kolm keys rotate [--kms <target>] [--overlap-days <N>]

Generates a fresh Ed25519 keypair and emits a rotation receipt with {old_key_fingerprint, new_key_fingerprint, rotated_at, overlap_until, kms_target}. Default overlap window is 30 days (NIST SP 800-57 Part 1 Rev 5 recommendation for high-assurance signing keys), customer-configurable. For --kms=local the new private PEM is written to disk at ~/.kolm/<fingerprint>.pem (mode 0600). For the four hosted KMS targets the wrap intent records api_status: awaiting_operator_hook so the customer KMS hook can act on the receipt without re-deriving anything. kolm emits the rotation receipt; the customer KMS hook applies the wrapping on its own infrastructure.

shipped · src/keys.js + cmdKeys in cli/kolm.js (wave 193)
shipped

kolm keys export --kms

Exports the active Ed25519 private key in a format the named KMS expects (PKCS8 PEM for AWS / GCP / Azure; HashiCorp Vault transit-engine import block for Vault). The export is the only moment the private key leaves the kolm signing store; everything after that is the KMS's responsibility, not kolm's.

primitive · today via cp ~/.kolm/signing-key.pem + KMS-native import

Customer-hosted KMS targets.

kolm does not host a KMS. The private signing key lives in the operator's KMS of choice; kolm exports the key in the format that KMS expects and pulls the key out at sign time via the KMS's standard SDK. This means the long-lived private key never sits on a kolm-controlled disk in steady state. Four targets are documented; the export format is portable PKCS8 PEM so any KMS that accepts standard Ed25519 keys works.

AWS KMS

AWS Key Management Service

Import the exported PKCS8 PEM into an AWS KMS customer-managed key with key spec ECC_NIST_P256 or use AWS KMS external-key-store for an Ed25519 key held in your own HSM. Sign at compile time via aws kms sign through the official SDK.

GCP KMS

GCP Cloud KMS

Import the exported PKCS8 PEM into a GCP Cloud KMS key ring as an Ed25519 asymmetric signing key (algorithm EC_SIGN_ED25519). Sign at compile time via the Cloud KMS asymmetric-sign API through google-cloud-kms.

Azure Key Vault

Azure Key Vault

Import the exported PKCS8 PEM into Azure Key Vault Managed HSM as an Ed25519 key (curve Ed25519); standard Key Vault tier does not yet GA Ed25519, so Managed HSM is the supported path. Sign via the Azure SDK KeyClient.sign.

HashiCorp Vault

HashiCorp Vault

Import the exported PEM into Vault's transit secrets engine with key type ed25519. Sign at compile time via vault write transit/sign/<key>. The transit-engine wrap-key flow keeps the key non-exportable after import.

What kolm ships vs what the customer owns.

Same shape as the drift+supersession honest-scope card on /drift: kolm gives the operator the primitives (signature scheme, key format, verifier semantics, public-key directory) and stays out of operations (key hosting, cadence policy, KMS choice). kolm does not fork a daemon, does not host a KMS, does not run a rotation cron. Every regulated tenant already has all three.

kolm ships

Signature scheme + key format + verifier semantics

  • Ed25519 signing primitive in src/ed25519.js (Wave H + Wave 149 default-on)
  • Public-key directory in src/pubkey-directory.js with proof-of-control challenge before any key registers
  • Manifest field signature_ed25519.key_fingerprint bound into artifact_hash_input so swapping keys after the fact breaks the Rekor anchor
  • Verifier check #5 (Ed25519 signature) and check #17 (policy require_ed25519) re-derive the signature offline from the embedded public key
  • CLI verbs kolm keygen, kolm pubkey, kolm keys list, kolm keys export --kms for the local-store lifecycle
customer owns

KMS hosting + rotation cadence + export targets

  • The KMS process: AWS KMS, GCP Cloud KMS, Azure Key Vault Managed HSM, HashiCorp Vault transit, or your own HSM
  • The rotation cadence policy: 365 days is the default kolm surfaces, but your compliance officer picks the number that satisfies your SOC 2 control, your NIST SP 800-57 target, or your jurisdiction-specific rule
  • The OS scheduler that triggers cadence-driven rotation: cron, systemd timer, k8s CronJob, Airflow DAG, GitHub Actions schedule
  • The incident-response playbook that fires reason=compromise rotations and the HR workflow that fires reason=personnel rotations
  • The KMS-native sign-time path at compile (so the private key never leaves the KMS after the one-time export)

Why no kolm-hosted KMS. The asymmetry of trust matters: a customer running kolm self-hosted who also stores their private signing key in a kolm-hosted KMS has handed kolm the ability to forge their signatures, which defeats the third-party-verifiable property the Ed25519 layer was added to provide (RS-1 §7.9). The same logic that keeps the PHI redactor in the customer-hosted compile bridge (/byoc) keeps the long-lived private key in the customer-hosted KMS. kolm ships the primitives and stays on the verify side of the trust boundary.
