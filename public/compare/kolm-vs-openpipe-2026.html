OpenPipe is a CoreWeave endpoint now. kolm is a file you own.

CoreWeave acquired OpenPipe in April 2026, folding the hosted fine-tuning product into CoreWeave's GPU cloud. OpenPipe still ships strong DPO/SFT training on managed infrastructure. kolm ships something different: a signed, portable artifact you can mail to a phone, run air-gapped, and verify with a third-party key. The two shapes are adjacent, not identical.

What OpenPipe ships in 2026 (under CoreWeave).

  • Hosted DPO/SFT/KTO on CoreWeave GPU infrastructure. Strong preference optimization, mixture-of-experts routing, batch fine-tuning workflows.
  • OpenAI-compatible endpoints served from CoreWeave regions. Drop-in replacement for an OpenAI SDK base URL.
  • Per-token serving billing with CoreWeave's GPU economics underneath. Volume discounts for committed throughput.
  • CoreWeave integration (April 2026+). Dedicated VPC SKU, enterprise procurement, MSA, and the CoreWeave compliance footprint (SOC 2, HIPAA-eligible under BAA).

What OpenPipe does not ship: a portable signed file you take with you when you change vendors, an air-gapped deployment without a network call to CoreWeave, a cryptographically verifiable receipt chain showing what teacher distilled the adapter, or a compiled native binary that runs on a phone or edge box.

The CoreWeave acquisition is real money meeting a real product.

April 2026: CoreWeave acquired OpenPipe. The combined product becomes hosted fine-tuning on a GPU-cloud-of-record. OpenPipe's customers gain the CoreWeave procurement and compliance posture; CoreWeave gains a managed fine-tuning product to sit on top of its raw GPU capacity.

This is good news for buyers who want a single throat to choke: training, serving, GPU economics, MSA, and compliance from one vendor. It does not change the gap that motivates kolm. CoreWeave + OpenPipe owns the runtime path. kolm owns the build artifact. An OpenAI-compatible endpoint cannot tell your auditor what data the train set held, whether the eval gate fired, what teacher distilled the model, or whether the bytes serving today match the bytes signed at compile time. Those are kolm's surface.

The two products work together. Build a signed adapter with kolm. Upload the LoRA weights to OpenPipe for hosted serving on CoreWeave. Verify the served adapter still matches the signed manifest with kolm verify. The signed file is the contract between the build team and the runtime team.

Extending OpenPipe with a signed kolm artifact.

# 1. Build the signed adapter with kolm
$ kolm distill support-triage \
    --teacher=anthropic:claude-opus-4-7 \
    --student-base=qwen2.5-7b \
    --redact=phi --eval-gate=0.85

  PASS  K-score 0.913 (A=0.94 T=0.91 F=0.95 R=0.88)
  PASS  PHI redactor: 18 Safe Harbor + 3 kolm extensions, no leakage
  PASS  Ed25519 signature, Rekor transparency log entry
  WROTE support-triage.kolm  (LoRA + manifest + receipt chain)

# 2. Extract the LoRA for OpenPipe upload
$ kolm extract support-triage.kolm --target=lora-weights --out=./adapter/

# 3. Upload to OpenPipe (now on CoreWeave) the way you already do
$ openpipe adapters upload ./adapter --base=qwen2.5-7b --name=support-triage

# 4. Verify the served adapter still matches what kolm signed
$ kolm verify support-triage.kolm
  PASS  24/24 checks · Ed25519 + Sigstore validated · receipt chain intact

When OpenPipe (on CoreWeave) is the right answer.

SaaS where every call is billable

You sell per-request inference and you want CoreWeave to own GPU operations. A hosted endpoint with per-token billing is the right shape; a signed file you mail customers is not.

Hosted DPO and preference data

You have a strong preference-data pipeline and want managed DPO/KTO without running training infrastructure. OpenPipe has been the cleanest implementation of that workflow for years.

One-vendor procurement story

Your procurement team wants a single MSA covering training, serving, and GPU compute. CoreWeave + OpenPipe is one vendor, one SOC 2, one BAA. That is genuinely valuable.

Scale-from-zero serving

Traffic is bursty and you do not want to provision your own GPUs. CoreWeave's underlying capacity gives OpenPipe more headroom than most managed-fine-tuning vendors carry.

When kolm is the right answer.

You ship to a phone, an edge box, a regulated VPC, or any environment where calling out to CoreWeave is a procurement problem. Your auditor needs cryptographic proof of what trained the model and that no raw PHI leaked into adapter weights. Your CFO needs flat compile cost rather than per-token serving forever. The signed .kolm file is the contract between the build team and whatever runtime you choose, including OpenPipe itself.
