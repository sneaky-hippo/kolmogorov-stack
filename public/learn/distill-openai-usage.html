Distill your OpenAI usage into a .kolm.

Capture prompt-response pairs from your live OpenAI traffic. Compile them into a specialist .kolm that runs offline. Replace one API call with a local execution that is 10-100× cheaper and free of egress.

Golden Path 2 of 3 . v1.0 . assumes you already completed step 1

What you will have. One .kolm trained on your actual prompts. K-score ≥ 0.85 against held-out cases from your own usage. Replaces a single OpenAI use-case at roughly 10-100× lower cost per call and zero data egress. Works on the same hardware you already own.

Why this works

You are already paying GPT-4 for narrow tasks like "summarize this support ticket" or "classify this email by intent." Each call is small, the answer space is narrow, and the prompts repeat. That is the exact shape kolm is built to compress. Capture your real usage. Distill it. Compile. The resulting .kolm handles the same task at a fraction of the cost.

step 1

Capture your live traffic.

2 minutes

kolm capture wrap installs a tiny shim that logs every OpenAI request and response to ~/.kolm/captures.jsonl. The shim is local; no data is sent to kolm.ai during capture. Set the same OPENAI_API_KEY you already use.

kolm capture wrap openai --env OPENAI_API_KEY
capture shim installed at ~/.kolm/shims/openai
add this to your code:
  import { openai } from '@kolm/capture'   # node
  from kolm_capture import openai          # python
or run with KOLM_CAPTURE=1 to enable shim-mode without code change.

Or, if you want zero code change, the shim can be installed as an HTTP proxy that your existing OpenAI SDK routes through:

kolm capture proxy --port 5310 --upstream https://api.openai.com
listening on :5310 . upstream api.openai.com . capturing to ~/.kolm/captures.jsonl
in your app, set OPENAI_BASE_URL=http://localhost:5310/v1 .
checkpoint use your app normally for one workday (or one hour of typical traffic). The capture file grows. No quality loss; every OpenAI call still goes through to OpenAI, you just keep a copy.
step 2

Inspect what you captured.

30 seconds 
kolm capture stats
captures:        1,402 prompts (~$11.30 spent on OpenAI today)
top intents:
  summarize-ticket             612 calls  (44%)
  classify-email-intent        388 calls  (28%)
  extract-shipping-address      174 calls  (12%)
  rewrite-tone                  142 calls  (10%)
  other                          86 calls  ( 6%)
files: ~/.kolm/captures.jsonl  (3.2 MB)

Pick one intent. The narrower the task, the better the distillation. summarize-ticket at 612 calls is a great first candidate; other at 86 calls is not.

step 3

Distill captures into a seed set.

60 seconds

kolm distill reads the capture file, filters by your task, optionally redacts PII before training, and emits a clean .seeds.jsonl file. The redaction step is mandatory if the captures include healthcare or PCI data; kolm distill refuses to write without it for those task tags.

kolm distill --task summarize-ticket --redact pii --out summarize.seeds.jsonl
distilled 612 captures -> 612 seed rows
  shape: {input, output}
  redaction: pii (names, emails, phones, SSN, addresses)
  estimated K-score upper bound: 0.93 (based on response consistency)
wrote summarize.seeds.jsonl (612 rows, 184 KB)
checkpoint the seed file is plain JSONL. Open it. Inspect the rows. Delete any you do not want to train on. The compile step trusts only what you keep.
step 4

Compile the specialist.

90 seconds

Use the same one-shot wrapper from Golden Path 1. The build verb derives a spec from the task and seeds, runs the compile pipeline, gates on K-score, and emits a signed artifact.

kolm build my-summarizer --spec-task "summarize support tickets in one sentence" --examples summarize.seeds.jsonl --yes
scaffolding spec my-summarizer.spec.json . ok
loading 612 seed rows from summarize.seeds.jsonl . ok
compiling . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .
K-score for my-summarizer.kolm: 0.917  (gate >= 0.85 - pass)
verifying signature . ok
artifact written: my-summarizer.kolm  (142 KB)

try it:
  kolm run my-summarizer.kolm --input <sample.json>
checkpoint 0.917 K-score against your own held-out cases. This is the artifact replacing your OpenAI calls.
step 5

Swap the API call.

90 seconds

Two ways to call your new artifact. CLI is good for batch. HTTP is good for drop-in replacement of the OpenAI SDK.

CLI batch. One call per line of input. Useful for offline reprocessing.

cat tickets.jsonl | kolm run my-summarizer.kolm

HTTP server. kolm serve exposes the artifact on a localhost port. Shape-compatible with OpenAI's /v1/chat/completions endpoint for the chat case, or a flat /v1/run for everything else.

kolm serve my-summarizer.kolm --port 5320 --openai-compat
listening on http://localhost:5320
  POST /v1/chat/completions   openai-compat shape
  POST /v1/run                native shape
artifact: my-summarizer.kolm  K=0.917  recipe=summarize-v1

In your code, change one URL:

// before
const openai = new OpenAI({ baseURL: "https://api.openai.com/v1" });

// after
const openai = new OpenAI({ baseURL: "http://localhost:5320/v1" });
checkpoint your app keeps working. The OpenAI bill stops growing for this use case. The latency drops. The data stops leaving your network.

What just happened

You converted ongoing variable cost ($) into a one-time fixed asset (the .kolm file). The artifact is yours, sits on your hardware, and runs without OpenAI in the loop. It cost you 5 minutes of capture, 5 minutes of compile, and zero infrastructure.

Repeat the same flow for each narrow OpenAI task in your app. Most teams find 60-80% of their OpenAI spend collapses into 5-10 specialist .kolm files. The remaining 20-40% (open-ended chat, complex reasoning) stays on GPT for now.

Quality, audited

kolm runs A/B checks against the captured OpenAI responses for the cases the model never trained on. The K-score is one number; the per-case verdict is in the receipt chain. Run kolm eval my-summarizer.kolm --examples holdout.jsonl any time to re-score against new captures. Drift detection is a kolm tune watch daemon away.
