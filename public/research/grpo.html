Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)

GRPO is an on-policy RL recipe that replaces PPO's learned value baseline with a within-group sample baseline. For each prompt, sample G completions, score them with a reward (verifiable or learned), normalize advantages relative to the group mean and standard deviation, then take a clipped policy-gradient step with KL regularization to a frozen reference. No value network, no critic, fewer hyperparameters than PPO.

What it is

GRPO was introduced in Shao et al. (DeepSeek-Math, 2024) and applied at scale in DeepSeek-R1 (DeepSeek-AI, 2025) to train mathematical reasoning. The objective is similar to PPO's clipped surrogate, but the advantage A_i for completion i in a group of G completions is computed as (r_i - mean(r_1..r_G)) / std(r_1..r_G), which removes the need for a separately trained value function. A KL penalty against a frozen reference policy controls drift; in the standard DeepSeek recipe this is the SFT-warmed checkpoint. The reward can be verifiable (math correctness, code unit tests, JSON-schema conformance) or learned (a process reward model or an LLM judge). Group sizes of 8 to 64 are typical. The recipe is cheaper than PPO at equivalent reasoning gains because the value network usually costs as much memory as the policy.

How kolm uses it

GRPO is not implemented in kolm v0.2.6. The shipped trainer covers SFT and response-level distillation only. GRPO is on the v0.3 roadmap as the optional post-distillation stage for buyers whose K-score has a verifiable component (regex match, JSON-schema conformance, unit-test pass). The integration will sample G=16 completions per prompt with the post-SFT student, score with the same K-score scorer the gate uses, and step with a small KL coefficient (typical: 0.04) to the SFT checkpoint. The receipt block will name the reward function, the group size, the clip range, the KL coefficient, and the post-RL holdout K-score. The /research/grpo-reasoning page covers a deeper dive into the verifiable-reward case; this stub covers the algorithm itself.
